My Dog, Bela
Bela is eight months old, born on June 19th 2020. She is an avid pinecone-chewer, and her favorite foods are peanuts and coconut flesh.

Heredity

DNA

DNA, also known as deoxyribonucleic acid, is the genetic information which instructs a dog in how to perform bodily functions. DNA is a double helix, formed of four nitrogenous bases: adenine, guanine, cytosine, and thymine. When linked together with a sugar and phosphate molecule, these bases produce proteins(which are a type of macromolecule).

The above process of making proteins is called protein synthesis. There are two parts to protein synthesis; the first is transcription.

During transcription, an RNA(ribonucleic acid) polymerase transcribes the DNA in the nucleus. It replaces the nitrogenous base thymine with uracil.

During translation, the RNA(mRNA) moves to the ribosomes in the cytoplasm. Then, the RNA is coded for amino acids. Every three bases is a codon, and these codons are translated into a complementary anticodon. The anticodon is then attached with an RNA(tRNA) to the correct amino acid. The sequence of the amino acids determines what protein is created.

The sections of DNA which code for molecules such as these proteins are called genes. There are around 19,000 protein coding genes. Genes are units of heredity which determine characteristics and traits, such as the color of a dog’s coat. These characteristics and traits create unique dogs which make up the population.

DNA is present in every cell of a dog, and contains 2.8 billion base pairs(made of the four nitrogenous bases). Unraveled, the DNA from one dog cell can be feet long. So how does the DNA fit into each cell? The answer is chromosomes. Found inside the nucleus of a cell(the “command center”), chromosomes tightly wound DNA around histones. Dogs have 78 chromosomes - or 39 pairs.

Reproduction

Bela is spayed, meaning her ovaries have been removed and therefore she cannot reproduce. Yet, if this was not so, a female dog such as Bela, would only be able to reproduce during her periods of heat. The “sexual “ cycle in female dogs has 4 phases, including two heat periods which last about 2-3 weeks each. The length of time between these heat periods depends on each dog.. Heat periods start once the females have reached sexual maturity. On the contrary, males have no “sexual” cycle, and are fertile when mature.

Genetics-wise, dogs reproduce sexually through meiosis. Meiosis is the process by which the reproductive cells are divided, and genetic variation occurs, with the result of the gametes(egg and sperm). Meiosis is similar to the process of mitosis(which is restricted to the somatic cells). Mitosis has multiple parts: prophase, metaphase, anaphase, telophase, and cytokinesis. In mitosis, the cell duplicates its DNA, and then splits into two genetically identical diploid daughter cells. Meiosis is the same process except twice, and resulting in four haploid gamete cells which are genetically unique. The resulting cells of meiosis are unique because in the meiosis one, genetic recombination happens. Genetic recombination is where the chromosomes of the biological parents are “exchanged”. This allows for genetic variation and therefore genetically unique dogs.

But why is genetic variation so important?

Genetic variation creates differences in the organisms of a species. These differences between organisms creates both advantages and disadvantages, causing competition. This competition allows some organisms to thrive and others to die, affecting the overall population size of the dogs which then affects the ecosystem. Without genetic variation, dogs would not be able to evolve and gain advantages, possibly leading to extinction.

Biochemistry

Macromolecules

Definition:

A macromolecule is a large organic molecule, with four main classes: carbohydrates, lipids, proteins, and nucleic acids.

Dogs especially need and derive their energy from proteins, fats, and carbohydrates. The energy that dogs get then is then "flowed" into other organisms via a food chain.

First, proteins:

Protein is absolutely critical to dogs. Specifically, dogs get ten essential amino acids from their protein that they cannot produce themselves. These amino acids are:

  • Arginine
  • Histidine
  • Isoleucine
  • Leucine
  • Lysine
  • Methionine
  • Phenylalanine
  • Threonine
  • Tryptophan
  • Valine

High-quality proteins are those with a balance of the above.

Second, fats:

Dietary fats provide the most concentrated energy, supplying fatty acids which are crucial in cell structure and functions such as mitosis. Fats improve the taste of dog food, and also aid in keeping a dog’s coat and skin healthy.

Third, carbohydrates:

Dogs are omnivorous animals, since they are secondary consumers, and often receive energy from carbohydrates - sugars, starches, fibers, etcetera. In dog food, carbohydrates can be consumed through legumes, cereals, and plant nutriment.

Cellular Respiration

Definition:

Cellular respiration is both an anaerobic and aerobic process by which glucose is broken down to create ATP.

ATP, also known as adenosine triphosphate, provides crucial energy for cell processes and function - for example meisois or protein synthesis.

Reactants and Products

The reactants of cellular respiration are the products of photosynthesis, being:

  • Glucose
  • Oxygen
Side note: Dogs do not do photosynthesis, or the process by which light energy is converted to chemical energy. Photosynthesis is only conducted by producers; while dogs are secondary consumers. Dogs get glucose from their food, and oxygen from breathing in the air.

The products of cellular respiration are the reactants of photosynthesis, being:

  • Carbon Dioxide
  • Water
  • ATP

The first stage of cellular respiration is glycolysis:

This is where glucose is split via glycolysis to become pyruvate. One glucose molecule equals two pyruvate molecules.

From this stage, there are two possible pathways. The first is fermentation:

During fermentation, a smaller amount of energy is produced since the process is anaerobic meaning oxygen is not utilized. There are two types of fermentation: lactic acid fermentation, of which the products are lactate, and alcoholic fermentation, of which the products are ethanol and carbon dioxide.

The second pathway is the Krebs cycle:

In dogs, the Krebs cycle takes place in the matrix of the mitochondria. It makes high energy molecules such as NADH. These high energy molecules are then used to power the electron transport chain. The electron transport chain is where the brunt of the energy happens and the majority of ATP is created. The above processes are aerobic, requiring oxygen, and therefore create much more energy than fermentation.

Ecology

The Transfer of Energy and Food Chains.

Energy flows through ecosystems, and every organism. Specifically, dogs:

Dogs get their energy through dog food, which is produced by humans. Energy from dogs is then passed through their fecal matter, which is then broken down by bacteria and other decomposers.

Role in the Ecosystem

So these bacteria which break down dogs’ fecal matter are decomposers, but what role does a dog have in an ecosystem?

Dogs are secondary consumers, because while they eat plants - and therefore, would be above plants(producers) - they also eat other animals which derive energy from plants(primary consumers). This means that a dog would be higher than both broccoli and cows in terms of the trophic levels(or hierarchical levels of an ecosystem).

Energy is lost at each trophic level as it is used by animals and plants to power cellular functions, such as meiosis.

Cycles of Matter

While energy is lost through its course in an ecosystem, matter is cycled through. These cycles of matter are referred to as biogeochemical cycles, and include any common type of matter which living things have. Three cycles are below:

The Water Cycle

Dogs receive water through:

  • Drinking Water
  • Water content from dog food

And expel water through:

  • Cellular respiration - water is a product of cellular respiration.
  • Waste products

Carbon Cycle

Dogs receive carbon through:

  • All dog food has macromolecules which contain carbon.
  • Drinking water(dissolved carbon dioxide is in water)

And expel carbon through:

  • Cellular respiration - carbon is a product of cellular respiration.
  • Waste products

Nitrogen Cycle

Dogs receive nitrogen through:

  • Dogs breathe nitrogen from the air.
  • Dogs also get nitrogen from the protein they consume from dog food.

And expel carbon through:

  • Waste products. These waste products rae decomposed by bacteria which converts the waste into ammonium. Nitrifying bacteria changes the ammonium into nitrate - which plants then use.

Trophic Cascades and Keystone Species

So how does the population of dogs affect the ecosystem?

If the population size of dogs decrease, there will be a greater amount of primary consumers since less will be eaten, and then a lesser amount of producers, since more producers will be eaten. However, the opposite happens if the dog population increases in size. However, in the status quo, domesticated dogs are dependent on human-produced dog food and have little to no interaction greatly affecting the ecosystem as a whole. Therefore, dogs are not a keystone species(or a species on which the environment greatly depends).

Populations and Pattern of Growth

The dog population in an ecosystem is dependent on a dog’s pattern of growth: K-strategists, which have few, protected offspring, thrive in a stable environment, and have a generally long life-span.

Dogs, via reproduction, give birth to 5-6 puppies, which is relatively small in comparison to other organisms, such as mosquitos which can produce 50-500 eggs. These puppies are protected, raised in a stable environment(which is now usually a home), and have a long life span; therefore dogs exhibit the traits of K-strategists.
